\subsection{Distributional Alignment With Humans}

Table~\ref{tab:ks} presents 2-sample Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) tests comparing
pooled LLM response distributions against human responses on the ordinal scale,
stratified by question type. Given the large sample sizes (model: 15,000--60,000;
human: 586--1373), all $P$ values were highly significant; the supremum
statistic $D$ was therefore used as the ranking criterion.

For perception questions, GPT-5.2 achieved the closest distributional match to
humans ($D$=0.070; $P$=.008), followed by GPT-4.1 ($D$=0.093).
Sonnet~4.5 was the most distributionally distant ($D$=0.324).
For information questions, Opus~4.6 was closest ($D$=0.131)
and Sonnet~4.5 again farthest ($D$=0.221).

The within-human split-half baseline ($D$$\approx$0.045 for perception;
$D$$\approx$0.028 for information; mean $P$$\approx$.82) represents
the intrinsic noise floor. \textbf{No model reached this floor}: even the
best-performing model (GPT-5.2 on perception, $D$=0.070) exceeded the
human baseline by a factor of approximately 1.5, indicating meaningful
headroom for future improvements.

\subsection{Stability of Estimates Across $N$=10 Iterations}

Table~\ref{tab:stability} reports the mean, SD, and standard error of the mean
(SEM) computed over the 10 independent iterations per model. SEM values were
uniformly small (all $\le$0.017; most $<$0.005), confirming that the
10-iteration average is a stable estimator of each model's expected behavior.
EAD exhibited approximately an order of magnitude lower variance than modal
MAE, consistent with EAD being a smooth functional of the response distribution
whereas modal MAE is a discrete plug-in statistic sensitive to mode flips.

\subsection{Normality Assessment}

Table~\ref{tab:shapiro} reports Shapiro-Wilk normality tests on the 10
per-iteration scores for each model. EAD scores satisfied the normality
assumption across all 4 models and both question types ($P$>.17),
supporting the use of parametric analysis of variance (ANOVA). Modal MAE
scores on perception questions violated normality for Sonnet~4.5
($W$=0.37; $P$<.001) and Opus~4.6 ($W$=0.51; $P$<.001);
ANOVA results for this metric-type combination should therefore be interpreted
with caution.

\subsection{Inter-Model Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:anova} reports one-way ANOVA across the 4 models (degrees of
freedom: 3, 36). All 4 metric-type combinations yielded highly significant
omnibus $F$ statistics ($F$$\ge$83; all $P$<$10^{-15}$), confirming
that the models differed significantly in their alignment performance.

Post hoc pairwise comparisons using Tukey honestly significant difference (HSD)
tests are shown in Table~\ref{tab:tukey}. A consistent pattern emerged:

\begin{itemize}\itemsep2pt
\item \textbf{Information-based questions:} Opus~4.6 statistically outperformed
  all other models on both modal MAE and EAD ($P_{\text{adj}}$<.001 for all
  comparisons), followed by GPT-5.2, GPT-4.1, and Sonnet~4.5 (ranked last).
\item \textbf{Perception-based questions:} GPT-5.2 achieved the lowest EAD
  (significantly better than all competitors), whereas Sonnet~4.5 achieved the
  lowest modal MAE. This discrepancy suggests that Sonnet~4.5 identifies the
  correct modal response but allocates probability mass less faithfully across
  the ordinal scale than GPT-5.2.
\end{itemize}
